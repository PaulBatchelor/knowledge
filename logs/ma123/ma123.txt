The rule goes "divide by the largest term in the denominator". Well, the largest term is
$\sqrt{25x^6} = \sqrt{25}\sqrt{x^6}$, so really we are dividing by
$\sqrt(x^6)$. This gets simplified to $x^3$, but there's a caveat
here. $\sqrt(x^6) = |x^3|$ the same way $\sqrt{x^2} = |x|$. If $x

$|x^3| = -x^3$ when $x \to -\infty$. So, for finding the limit $x
\to -\infty$, the numerator is still being divided by $\sqrt(x^6)$
like the rule, but now $\sqrt{x^6}=|x^3|=-x^3$.

@10:19 roots, powers, and fractions are mixed notation #scratch:02s
#! dz bu/ma123/problems/3_4_56
I put this problem on my curated problem set list because of the work I had to do to obtain an answer the "long way" (using the quotient rule instead of simplifying). It turns out, this problem became a lot simpler once I replaced all the roots with powers. This leads me to an insight: using expressions with both roots and powers are mixed notation, which makes it more difficult to solve the problem because you are working with two different systems.
---
The advantage of writing something like $\sqrt(x)$ as $x^{1/2}$ is that there is more stuff you can do in this form. You have laws of exponents in your toolkit, AND you have laws of fractions.
---
In an alternate timeline, we could be living in a world where root notation was more popular.
You could write ${y \over x}$ as
$y x^{-1} = y {\root -1 \of x}$, or even
${\root 1 \of y}{\root -1 \of x}$. We are used to seeing things like $x^{1/2} = \sqrt{x}$, but certainly this would imply $x^2 = {\root (1/2) \of x}$? Still haven't thought this through, but you could probably rewrite fractions to be in terms of root, and derive all the conventional power laws to be in terms of roots (call them "root laws").
