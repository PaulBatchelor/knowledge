I've outlining and thinking about this one for a while.
---
Here is my essay:
---
In the spirit of leaving things in a better place than when I found
them, I'd like to offer some constructive criticism for CS-248.
There are two key areas that I believe could use some improvement,
and I'd like to offer some suggestions for improvement.
---
The first key area of improvement is structure. Prof. Shahossini
made it very clear from the beginning that he wanted to make this
class flexible enough so as to adapt to the pace of the class. I
respect this choice very much. However, there is a double edged
sword here: make it too flexible, and you lose structural integrity.
I would argue that an empty syllabus is too much flexibility,
especially for a subject like mathematics. It was never clear when
we had to do homework, or if we had to do homework. I struggled
with quizes and midterms, despite having studied for them and having
it be open book. For example, one particular quiz introduced entirely
new terms without any definition which could not be found anywhere
in any chapter of the textbook (let alone the assigned chapter),
which I found unfair and disproportionately compared to the
expectations I had come to understand from the class. Challenging
exams are a hallmark of university-level math courses, but the
difference between those courses and this one is that these students
are well supported; a typically undergraduate mathematics course
has TAs and official office hours. We were given neither.
---
My suggestion to improve the overall structure is to make CS-248
more of what is trying to be: a remedial course on discrete mathematics
using rote learning. In rote learning, there is a contract between
teachers and students: follow the textbook, and you will succeed.
For this to work, the textbook (in this case, zybooks) must be a
source of truth. I believe there will be a significant bump in
student performance if the tests and quizes more closely resembled
the problems and assignments we did in homework. Zybooks has plenty
of exercises to choose from. Choose a couple from the textbook,
maybe modify them a little bit, and that's your quiz.
---
The second area of improvement is classroom environment. To put it
bluntly: it was hostile. On the very first day of class, Professor
Shahossini blew up at a student for asking a clarifying question.
The student made the mistake of saying the professor was wrong,
citing the textbook that he had just downloaded minutes ago. Instead
of trying to foster a discussion, the Professor called it a disruption,
and proceeded to get into a shouting match for 15 minutes. This
pattern occurred again a few classes later, where a student found
a mistake on the board (a counter-example to what was supposedly a
valid argument), and proceeded to spend the rest of class trying
to prove that the student was incorrect. I do not know if the student
was correct. What I do know is this: it took up the rest of the
class, we went overtime (the only time this ever happened), and the
manner of proving him wrong (building up a massive truth table),
was precisely what we were told not to do minutes prior. The class
after our midterm, the Professor was visibly very unsettled and
upset, and took it out on the class by yelling at us as went over
the midterm. I have not had a teacher this volatile in about 20
years. I've been very anxious coming into classes.
---
I want to make something very, very, clear: Professor Shahossini
is a kind man. All my interactions outside of class have been lovely.
He remembers my name, takes an interest in my life, and genuinely
seems passionate about teaching. On these metrics, he has been an
excellent professor, better than all the professors I've had up to
this point during my time at CSMET.
---
So, what is going on here? Why is a kind man yelling at a class of
students? I can only make guesses based on observation. I do know
he works 6 full days a week. We have a night class that ends at
8:45pm after a full day of classes, and it's not even his last class
of the day. This man is overworked. How could anyone teach with
those hours? In the industry, we would call this burnout. The first
day of class when he got into a heated argument with a student, I
recall he was also late to class. His reason for the tardiness had
to do with his attempt to get a replacement laptop, which apparently
was a struggle. I know he also had to move a fair bit to get to
this classroom, and wanted to switch classrooms (we never switched
classrooms, so either the request was never made, or the request
was denied). Things like this lead me to ask: is the school
administration providing the necessary resources for professors to
succeed? Are they treating professors well? If the teachers are
unable to teach, the students are unable to learn; this is a logical
implication I learned in CS-248. What is my motivation for giving
money to this administration if they have failed to support the
educators which form the backbone of the institution? If you are
an administrator, my plea is this: take care of your teachers. They
are precious.
---
